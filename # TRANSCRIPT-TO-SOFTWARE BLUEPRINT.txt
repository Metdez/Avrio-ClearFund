## SECTION 2: THE PROBLEM IN ONE PARAGRAPH

This is a 14-month-old private credit startup that connects borrowers (hotel developers, infrastructure companies, mining operations) with capital providers (banks, asset managers, family offices, life insurance companies) while blending traditional commercial real estate financing with public financing instruments like PACE. Their current CRM (Zoho) treats relationships as a simple linear pipeline — lead to client to converted — but their business has two entirely separate sides (borrowers and capital providers) with overlapping, multi-threaded relationships: the same capital provider might reject one deal, be evaluating a second deal, and simultaneously be negotiating a long-term credit facility partnership, and Zoho cannot represent any of this. After deals are agreed upon, the 6-to-9-week execution phase requires coordinating 8 to 15 third-party vendors (appraisers, law firms, energy consultants, municipalities) with no centralized status tracking, no standardized process, and no visibility across the team — everyone has their own idea of how it should be done. If nothing changes, deal execution will remain chaotic, relationship context will continue to live in individual inboxes rather than a shared system, and the team will keep relying on Zoho to solve problems it was never designed for.

## SECTION 4: WHAT THE SOFTWARE MUST DO (CORE FEATURES)

### F-001: Dual-Sided Relationship Management (Borrowers + Capital Providers)

- **Priority:** P0
- **What the client said:** "We have borrowers on one side. Then on the other side we have the actual capital providers... It's really — CRMs kind of struggle with our business. We're not just managing lead to client to converted clients. We have borrowers on one side and we have capital providers on the other side."
- **Translated to a clear requirement:** The system must maintain two distinct entity types — Borrowers and Capital Providers — each with their own profiles, relationship histories, and pipeline stages. These two sides must be linkable through Deals (a Deal connects a Borrower to one or more Capital Providers). The system must not force both sides into a single linear sales pipeline.
- **Who uses this feature:** All user types
- **What triggers this feature:** User creates or views a Borrower or Capital Provider record
- **Expected output:** A borrower profile showing all associated deals and status; a capital provider profile showing all associated deals, credit facility status, and relationship type (one-off vs. programmatic partner)
- **Acceptance Criteria:**
  1. A user can create a Borrower record and a Capital Provider record as distinct entity types
  2. A Deal record can be linked to one Borrower and one or more Capital Providers
  3. Viewing a Capital Provider shows every deal they are or have been involved in, plus any credit facility negotiations
- **Dependencies:** None — this is foundational
- **Open Questions:** What specific fields are needed on Borrower vs. Capital Provider profiles? What are the pipeline stages for each side?

### F-002: Multi-Thread Relationship Tracking per Capital Provider

- **Priority:** P0
- **What the client said:** "We might be talking to one capital provider about five different deals or three different deals along with a JV partnership as a credit facility. That becomes complicated sometimes... Let's say we're currently negotiating a credit facility with [a provider]. But then on the side, there's also a singular deal that [they] really like. And in the interim, they want to fund that deal and see where it goes from there. And maybe we'll sign the paperwork for the credit facility two months from now. That's kind of annoying for a CRM to decipher between."
- **Translated to a clear requirement:** The system must allow multiple concurrent "engagement threads" per Capital Provider. Each thread represents a distinct business context: a specific deal evaluation, a credit facility negotiation, or a JV partnership discussion. Each thread must have its own status, conversation history, and timeline — while all threads roll up to a single Capital Provider profile for a unified relationship view.
- **Who uses this feature:** Deal Team, Capital Raising Team, CEO
- **What triggers this feature:** User opens a Capital Provider record and views or creates engagement threads
- **Expected output:** A Capital Provider profile page showing all active and historical engagement threads with their respective statuses, timelines, and conversation summaries
- **Acceptance Criteria:**
  1. A Capital Provider can have multiple simultaneous engagement threads (e.g., Deal A evaluation + Credit Facility negotiation)
  2. Each thread has its own status, notes, and conversation history
  3. A unified timeline view shows all interactions across threads in chronological order
  4. Users can filter by thread type (deal-specific, credit facility, JV partnership)
- **Dependencies:** F-001 (Dual-Sided Relationship Management)
- **Open Questions:** How many concurrent threads does a typical capital provider have? What are the status stages for credit facility negotiations vs. deal evaluations?

### F-003: Capital Provider Type Classification (One-Off vs. Credit Facility Partner)

- **Priority:** P0
- **What the client said:** "We have capital providers [where] a lot of them work with us for just like one deal. And we have other capital providers that are effectively partners of ours that have programmatic [arrangements]... So they basically say to us, we'll open up our piggy bank for you. You have access to 200 million dollars a year. These are the terms... Versus sometimes we'll do a deal where the capital provider... they're just in that one deal and then that's it."
- **Translated to a clear requirement:** The system must classify Capital Providers into at least two categories: (1) Transactional — involved in a single deal with no ongoing programmatic relationship, and (2) Credit Facility Partner — has a formal or in-progress credit facility agreement with defined terms (dollar amount, spread split, refinancing provisions). The system must store credit facility terms and link them to all deals funded under that facility.
- **Who uses this feature:** Deal Team, Capital Raising Team
- **What triggers this feature:** User categorizes a Capital Provider during creation or updates their classification as the relationship evolves
- **Expected output:** Capital Provider records clearly labeled by type, with Credit Facility Partners showing facility terms, utilization, and associated deals
- **Acceptance Criteria:**
  1. Every Capital Provider record has a relationship type field (Transactional, Credit Facility Partner, Prospective Partner, or other)
  2. Credit Facility Partner records include fields for facility size, term split, spread allocation, and refinancing provisions
  3. The system can show how much of a credit facility has been utilized across deals
- **Dependencies:** F-001
- **Open Questions:** What specific terms are tracked in a credit facility agreement? Are there other relationship types beyond transactional and credit facility (e.g., JV partner, co-lender)? John mentioned life insurance companies as a category — is that a distinct type?

### F-005: Deal Execution / Post-Agreement Task Management

All team members must be able to see the status of every task in real time.
- **Who uses this feature:** Deal Team
- **What triggers this feature:** A deal moves from "Agreed" to "Execution" status
- **Expected output:** A deal execution dashboard with a checklist of all required tasks, their assignments, statuses, and deadlines — with a progress indicator showing overall completion
- **Acceptance Criteria:**
  1. A deal in execution phase displays a configurable task checklist
  2. Each task has an assignee, status (not started / in progress / blocked / complete), and due date
  3. All team members with access can see real-time task status
  4. Tasks can be assigned to third-party vendors (by name/company, even if they don't have system accounts)
  5. The system flags overdue tasks
- **Dependencies:** F-004
- **Open Questions:** What is the standard checklist of tasks for deal execution? ## SECTION 6: USER WORKFLOWS (HOW PEOPLE ACTUALLY USE IT)

### Workflow 1: New Borrower Deal Origination

- **Trigger:** A borrower approaches the firm about financing a project (hotel, infrastructure, mining, etc.)
- **Step 1:** User creates a Borrower record (or selects existing) and creates a new Deal record linked to that Borrower
- **Step 2:** User enters deal details — project type, location, estimated deal size, financing structure (% traditional mortgage vs. % PACE/public financing)
- **Step 3:** System places the deal in the origination pipeline at the first stage
- **Step 4:** User identifies and links appropriate Capital Providers to pitch
- **Step 5:** For each Capital Provider pitched, system creates an engagement thread on that Capital Provider's record
- **Step 6:** User tracks responses — Capital Provider says yes, no, or needs more info
- **Step 7:** When a Capital Provider commits, deal moves to "Agreed" and triggers the execution workflow
- **End State:** Deal has a committed Capital Provider and is ready for execution
- **Error States:** Capital Provider says no (deal needs to be pitched to others); Borrower withdraws; PACE/public financing program is not available in the municipality (this is a real risk — John described this happening on a current deal)

### Workflow 2: Deal Execution (Post-Agreement)

- **Trigger:** Deal moves from "Agreed" to "Execution" phase
- **Step 1:** System auto-generates the execution task checklist from the process template (appraisal, legal, energy study, municipal approval, etc.)
- **Step 2:** User assigns each task to a responsible party (internal team member or third-party vendor name)
- **Step 3:** As tasks are completed, users update status; system shows overall progress
- **Step 4:** System flags overdue tasks and optionally triggers automated follow-ups
- **Step 5:** When all tasks are complete, deal moves to "Ready to Fund"
- **Step 6:** Deal is funded; system records closing details
- **End State:** Deal is funded and closed; all documentation is stored in the deal record
- **Error States:** Vendor is unresponsive (follow-up automation triggers); municipality rejects approval (deal may need to be restructured or killed); appraisal comes in below expected value; deal "blows up" (John's words — needs a termination workflow)

### Workflow 3: Credit Facility Negotiation

- **Trigger:** Team identifies a Capital Provider as a potential credit facility partner
- **Step 1:** User creates a Credit Facility engagement thread on the Capital Provider's record
- **Step 2:** User tracks negotiation stages — initial conversation, terms proposed, terms negotiated, legal review, signed
- **Step 3:** When facility is signed, user records facility terms (dollar amount, spread split, refinancing provisions, duration)
- **Step 4:** Future deals funded under this facility are linked to it; system tracks utilization
- **End State:** Active credit facility with terms recorded and utilization tracked
- **Error States:** Negotiation stalls; terms change; facility is terminated

### Workflows Not Discussed But Likely Needed:

1. **Deal Cancellation / Kill** — John mentioned a current deal might "blow up." There is no described workflow for what happens when a deal dies mid-execution.
2. **Capital Provider Onboarding** — When a new capital provider is identified, what information is collected? Is there a due diligence process?
3. **Reporting / Dashboard Refresh** — How does leadership consume pipeline data? No workflow described for generating or reviewing reports.
4. **User Onboarding / Permissions** — No discussion of how new team members are added or what access levels they get.

## SECTION 18: GLOSSARY

- **PACE (C-PACE):** Property Assessed Clean Energy — a public financing mechanism that allows property owners to finance energy-efficient and sustainable building improvements through long-term, low-interest debt that is assessed on the property. Requires municipality opt-in.
- **Credit Facility:** A formal agreement with a capital provider that gives the firm access to a defined pool of capital (e.g., $200M/year) under pre-agreed terms, allowing them to fund multiple deals without negotiating each one individually.
- **Capital Provider:** Any entity that provides money for deals — includes banks, asset managers (Fortress, Aries Capital Management), family offices, and life insurance companies.
- **Borrower:** The entity seeking financing — could be a hotel developer, infrastructure company, mining operation, or any commercial real estate project.
- **Credit Facility Partner vs. Transactional Capital Provider:** A Credit Facility Partner has an ongoing programmatic relationship with pre-agreed terms. A Transactional Capital Provider participates in a single deal with no ongoing commitment.
- **Spread:** The margin/fee added on top of the base interest rate on a loan. The spread split (e.g., 60/40) defines how origination revenue is shared between the firm and the capital provider.
- **Origination:** The process of sourcing, structuring, and funding a new deal.
- **Underwriting:** The process of evaluating the risk and terms of a potential deal.
- **JV (Joint Venture):** A partnership between two or more parties for a specific business purpose. The firm itself was formed as a JV with Wedbush Securities.
- **Wedbush Securities:** A Los Angeles-headquartered investment bank that is the firm's JV partner. Provides office space and some infrastructure.
- **SIE (Securities Industry Essentials):** An entry-level FINRA exam — discussed in the transcript in the context of career advice, not relevant to the software.
- **Loan-on-Loan Financing:** When a private credit firm borrows money from a bank to then lend it out to borrowers — the structure described by John as the origin of private credit.
- **RIA:** Registered Investment Advisor — a type of financial firm. Referenced when John discussed Red Tail CRM (used by wealth management RIAs), not directly relevant to this build.
- **Go High Level:** A CRM/marketing automation platform that Zack used for a previous client (tax resolution company). Referenced as a comparable, not as a tool for this project.
- **Linear:** A project management tool for software development. Zack uses it internally for his company Licom AI. Not proposed for the client's use.
